# Copywriting Rules

Read this when generating customer-facing text, placeholder content, CTAs, labels, headings, testimonials, pricing, FAQs, page sections, alt text, microcopy, or review feedback on copy.

Copy is part of the UI. Write it in the layout context, not as detached filler.

## Non-Negotiable Character Rules

| Banned | Use instead |
| --- | --- |
| Em dash character | Period, comma, colon, parentheses, or a rewritten sentence |
| En dash for joining words | Hyphen or the word "to" |
| Curly or smart quotes | Straight quotes |
| Ellipsis character | Three periods only when the voice truly trails off |

Use plain ASCII punctuation unless the user provides final brand copy that already uses different punctuation.

## The "Whether X or Y" Rule

Never use the construction "Whether you're a [persona A] or [persona B]."

Bad:

> Whether you're a freelancer or an enterprise team, our platform scales with you.

Good:

> Teams of 2 and teams of 200 use the same tool. It grows with you.

If audience segmentation matters, use separate sections, separate cards, or direct statements.

## Write With Purpose

Most sentences happen because they sound like what should be written. Do not write by default.

Bad:

> We're excited to announce the future of photography.

Good:

> The industry formerly known as "photography" has ceased to exist.

If the first version sounds like writing, rewrite it. The goal is copy that sounds like someone wrote it that way on purpose.

## Provocation

Provocation works only when it comes from the product. Provocation for its own sake is noise.

Examples:

- "Refresh your thirst" becomes "Murder your thirst" for Liquid Death.
- "Buy this jacket" becomes "Don't buy this jacket" for Patagonia.
- "Party game for friends" becomes "...horrible people" for Cards Against Humanity.

To find the angle:

1. Write down what everyone in the category says.
2. Pick one part to break.
3. Generate bad ideas first.
4. Keep only the provocation that explains the product better.

## The Three Laws Of Great Copy

Every important sentence must pass at least two of these three tests. If it passes all three, it ships.

### 1. Can I Visualize It?

People remember pictures. Concrete details create pictures.

| Abstract | Visual |
| --- | --- |
| "Productive routines" | "Waking up at 6am to write" |
| "Worn by supermodels and dads" | "Worn by supermodels in London and dads in Ohio" |
| "Fast delivery" | "On your doorstep before your coffee gets cold" |

Add specific places, times, objects, people, numbers, and outcomes.

### 2. Can I Falsify It?

Vague claims are invisible because they cannot be proven wrong.

| Weak | Strong |
| --- | --- |
| "We have a harmonious connection with quality" | "Every bag is stitched by one person, start to finish" |
| "Best-in-class performance" | "Loads in 0.8 seconds on 3G" |
| "Trusted by thousands" | "11,342 teams signed up this quarter" |

### 3. Can Nobody Else Say This?

If a competitor could write the sentence, cut it.

Bad:

> The Cybertruck is the world's best truck.

Good:

> The Cybertruck is tougher than an F150 and faster than a Porsche.

## Conversational Copy Rules

- Write with the reader, not at the reader.
- Use "you," "your," "we," and "our" when appropriate.
- Use contractions in customer-facing copy.
- Start sentences with "And," "But," "Or," or "So" when it improves flow.
- Do not persuade loudly. State facts, show proof, tell stories, and let the reader decide.
- Do not imitate phrases seen on ten other landing pages.
- Use the simple word first.
- Use customer language from reviews, tickets, calls, and objections.
- Respect competitors. Do not trash them.
- If the copy feels like it is trying too hard, rewrite it.
- Read it aloud. If it sounds like a real person, keep it.

## Mechanics

- Be specific. "Saves time" becomes "Cut report generation from 4 hours to 12 minutes."
- Be direct. "You get daily backups" beats "Daily backups are provided to ensure your data remains safe."
- Use short sentences. Split anything over 25 words.
- Use active voice. "We ship updates weekly" beats "Updates are shipped weekly."
- Use plain words. "Use" not "utilize." "Help" not "empower." "Start" not "embark."
- One idea per sentence.
- Remove qualifier stacking. "Very unique" becomes "unique" or a concrete detail.
- Avoid gerund headlines. "Building better apps" is weaker than "Build better apps."
- Use numbers over adjectives. "Loads in 0.8 seconds" beats "fast."
- Delete until every word earns its place.

## Landing Page Copy Structure

When generating a full landing page or multi-section page, follow this structure unless the user requests another structure.

### Above The Fold

The reader should understand the offer in five seconds.

1. Title: explain the value.
   - Explain what the product does when clarity is enough.
   - Use a hook when the category is crowded.
   - Own the niche in one line when the product has a clear position.
2. Subtitle: explain how the product creates that value.
3. Visual: show the product, process, result, proof, or scene. Avoid generic illustrations and handshake imagery.
4. Social proof: logos, testimonial snippets, customer counts, visible usage, or specific outcomes.
5. CTA: one clear next step.

### Below The Fold

1. Features and objections: make the promised value concrete.
2. More proof: use customers, numbers, before-and-after details, or real usage stories.
3. FAQ: answer loose ends and objections.
4. Second CTA: repeat the call to action with context.
5. Founder note or story: use only when it makes the offer easier to remember.

Landing page test:

> Would this element help sell the product if the founder met the customer in person?

If not, remove it.

## CTA And Button Copy

| Weak | Strong | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| "Get Started Today!" | "Start free" | Calm, direct, no fake urgency |
| "Learn More" | "See pricing" or "Read the docs" | Tells the reader what happens next |
| "Submit" | "Send message" or "Place order" | Describes the action |
| "Click Here" | "Download the report" | Describes the destination |
| "Sign Up Now!" | "Create account" | Specific and calm |
| "Start Trial" | "See your analytics" | Leads with value |

Add objection-handling text near CTAs when appropriate:

- "Start free. No credit card."
- "Book a 15-minute walkthrough."
- "Download the checklist. No email required."

## Headlines

- Make a promise.
- Write the headline in the layout context.
- Check line breaks at mobile and desktop widths.
- Do not isolate copy from the design. Visual weight changes meaning.

## Pricing Copy

When generating pricing sections:

1. Make the recommended tier visually dominant.
2. Show savings concretely, not as "save more."
3. Use tier names that set expectations: Starter, Pro, Team, Studio, Clinic, Agency.
4. Avoid abstract tier names that need explanation.
5. Avoid landing-page words in tier descriptions.

## Placeholder And Demo Content

- Write realistic copy that could ship.
- Do not use lorem ipsum unless requested.
- Match the implied industry.
- Use specific names, roles, numbers, dates, objections, and locations.
- Alt text must describe the image content meaningfully.
- Use customer words whenever possible.

## Microcopy

- Error messages state what went wrong and what to do next.
  - Example: "That email address is already registered. Try logging in instead."
- Empty states say what belongs there and how to create it.
  - Example: "No projects yet. Create your first one to start tracking quotes."
- Loading states should prefer skeleton screens or progress context over generic "Loading..."
- Form labels use nouns.
  - Good: "Email address"
  - Weak: "Enter your email address"

## Differentiation Test

Before shipping copy, ask:

> Could my competitor have written this sentence?

If yes, rewrite it using product-specific details, proof, mechanism, customer language, or a sharper point of view.
